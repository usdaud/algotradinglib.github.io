# Net Unrealized Appreciation (NUA)

Net Unrealized Appreciation (NUA) is a term primarily used in the context of [tax planning](../t/tax_planning.md) and retirement accounts, particularly those that involve employer-sponsored stock, like company stock held in a [401(k) plan](../1/401(k)_plan.md). NUA refers to the increase in [value](../v/value.md) of company stock while it is held in a retirement plan. It is an important concept for individuals who [hold](../h/hold.md) employer securities in their qualified retirement accounts since it can have significant tax implications when the stock is distributed from the plan.

### Key Concepts

#### Definition
Net Unrealized Appreciation (NUA) specifically refers to the appreciation of the original [value](../v/value.md) of company stock in a retirement account. This can have considerable tax advantages if the stock has appreciated significantly since it was contributed to the plan.

#### NUA Calculation
To calculate NUA, you need to determine the original [value](../v/value.md) of the employer stock at the time it was contributed to the retirement plan (the [cost basis](../c/cost_basis.md)) and its [value](../v/value.md) when it is distributed from the plan. The difference between these two values is the NUA.

**Formula:**
```
NUA = [Value](../v/value.md) of Stock at [Distribution](../d/distribution.md) - [Original Cost](../o/original_cost.md) [Basis](../b/basis.md)
```

#### Tax Implications
One of the primary reasons NUA is important is due to its favorable tax treatment. When stock is distributed from a retirement plan, the [cost basis](../c/cost_basis.md) is taxed as [ordinary income](../o/ordinary_income.md). However, the NUA is taxed at long-term [capital](../c/capital.md) gains rates when the stock is sold, which are typically lower than [ordinary income](../o/ordinary_income.md) tax rates.

1. **[Ordinary Income](../o/ordinary_income.md) Tax**: The [cost basis](../c/cost_basis.md) of the stock is included as [ordinary income](../o/ordinary_income.md) in the year of [distribution](../d/distribution.md).
2. **Long-Term [Capital](../c/capital.md) Gains**: The NUA is subject to long-term [capital gains tax](../c/capital_gains_tax.md) when the stock is sold, regardless of the [holding period](../h/holding_period.md) after the [distribution](../d/distribution.md).

#### Benefits and Considerations
- **Tax Savings**: The main benefit of NUA is the potential for significant tax savings, as long-term [capital gains tax](../c/capital_gains_tax.md) rates are generally lower than [ordinary income](../o/ordinary_income.md) tax rates.
- **Lump Sum [Distribution](../d/distribution.md)**: To take advantage of NUA, the [distribution](../d/distribution.md) of company stock must be a lump-sum [distribution](../d/distribution.md) from the retirement account, which means the entire balance of the account must be distributed within a single tax year.
- **Subsequent Appreciation**: Any subsequent appreciation in the stock after it is distributed [will](../w/will.md) also be subject to [capital gains tax](../c/capital_gains_tax.md), but the [holding period](../h/holding_period.md) for determining whether it qualifies for long-term or short-term treatment starts anew after the [distribution](../d/distribution.md).
- **[Diversification](../d/diversification.md)**: Individuals need to weigh the benefits of NUA tax treatment against the need for proper [diversification](../d/diversification.md) to mitigate investment [risk](../r/risk.md).

### Steps to Implement NUA Strategy

1. **Evaluate Employer Stock**: Determine if you have appreciated employer stock in your 401(k) or similar retirement plan.
2. **Consult a [Financial Advisor](../f/financial_advisor.md)**: Speak with a [financial advisor](../f/financial_advisor.md) or tax professional to understand if taking advantage of NUA is the best strategy based on your specific financial situation.
3. **Lump-Sum [Distribution](../d/distribution.md)**: Plan to take a lump-sum [distribution](../d/distribution.md) of the retirement account including the employer stock.
4. **Separate [Distribution](../d/distribution.md)**: Ensure the employer stock is distributed in-kind to a taxable [brokerage account](../b/brokerage_account.md), keeping the rest of the retirement account separate.
5. **Track [Cost Basis](../c/cost_basis.md) and NUA**: Record the [cost basis](../c/cost_basis.md) and NUA of the distributed stock for tax reporting purposes.
6. **Sell Stock Strategically**: Decide when to sell the stock to maximize long-term [capital](../c/capital.md) gains benefits, keeping in mind [market](../m/market.md) conditions and personal financial needs.

### Example Scenario

Consider an individual, Jim, who holds employer stock in his [401(k) plan](../1/401(k)_plan.md) with a [cost basis](../c/cost_basis.md) of $50,000, and the current [market value](../m/market_value.md) of the stock is $200,000 at the time of [distribution](../d/distribution.md).

1. **[Cost Basis](../c/cost_basis.md)**: $50,000 [will](../w/will.md) be taxed as [ordinary income](../o/ordinary_income.md) in the year of [distribution](../d/distribution.md).
2. **NUA**: $150,000 ($200,000 - $50,000) [will](../w/will.md) be eligible for long-term [capital gains tax](../c/capital_gains_tax.md) treatment when the stock is eventually sold.

If Jim is in the 24% [federal income](../f/federal_income.md) tax bracket for [ordinary income](../o/ordinary_income.md) and the long-term [capital gains tax](../c/capital_gains_tax.md) rate is 15%, the tax implications would be:
- **[Ordinary Income](../o/ordinary_income.md) Tax**: \( $50,000 \times 24\% = $12,000 \)
- **Long-Term [Capital Gains Tax](../c/capital_gains_tax.md)**: \( $150,000 \times 15\% = $22,500 \)

### Practical Considerations

- **Eligibility for NUA**: Not all employer plans allow for NUA treatment, so it is crucial to verify this ahead of time.
- **Age and Early [Distribution](../d/distribution.md) Penalties**: Distributions before age 59½ may be subject to an additional 10% early [distribution](../d/distribution.md) penalty, though NUA distributions can sometimes avoid this penalty.
- **Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)**: Once you reach age 72, required minimum distributions can complicate NUA strategies, so planning ahead is necessary to align NUA with RMD requirements.
