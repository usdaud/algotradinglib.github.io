# Volume Spread Analysis (VSA)

[Volume](../v/volume.md) [Spread Analysis](../s/spread_analysis.md) (VSA) is a trading method that seeks to predict [market](../m/market.md) direction by examining the relationship between [volume](../v/volume.md), price, and the spread of a [security](../s/security.md)'s price. This technique was originally based on the ideas put forward by Richard D. Wyckoff, a prominent early 20th century [trader](../t/trader.md) and [market](../m/market.md) analyst. VSA delves deeper into the interrelationship between these [market](../m/market.md) variables, aiming to uncover the activities of professional traders, [market](../m/market.md) makers, and other large operators, also known as the "[Smart Money](../s/smart_money.md)."

## Core Principles of VSA

### 1. Volume
[Volume](../v/volume.md) represents the number of [shares](../s/shares.md) or contracts traded during a specific period (e.g., minute, hour, day). It is a crucial element in VSA because it reveals the intensity or activity level in the [market](../m/market.md). High [volume](../v/volume.md) indicates strong [interest](../i/interest.md) and considerable activity, whether from buyers or sellers, while low [volume](../v/volume.md) suggests lower [interest](../i/interest.md) or activity.

### 2. Price
Price refers to the [security](../s/security.md)'s trading levels, typically represented through charts. In VSA, price movements are analyzed in conjunction with [volume](../v/volume.md) to identify potential setups and trends. Key price points, such as [support and resistance](../s/support_and_resistance.md) levels, are investigated to determine the strength or weakness of the [market](../m/market.md).

### 3. Spread
The spread in VSA indicates the difference between the high and low of a single price bar. It's a measure of the [range](../r/range.md) of the price movement within a given trading period. A wide spread, combined with high or low [volume](../v/volume.md), can provide valuable insights into the [market](../m/market.md)â€™s direction and the behavior of professional traders.

## Implementing VSA in Trading

### 1. Accumulation Phase
This phase occurs when professional traders begin to buy (accumulate) large amounts of a [security](../s/security.md) at lower prices. During this phase, the [market](../m/market.md) is typically quiet, with low [volatility](../v/volatility.md) and small price movements, as large operators build their positions without attracting attention.

### 2. Mark-Up Phase
Once professionals have accumulated enough positions, they push prices higher, resulting in a mark-up phase. This phase is characterized by rising prices accompanied by increasing [volume](../v/volume.md), signaling strong buying [interest](../i/interest.md) from professionals and eventually attracting retail traders.

### 3. Distribution Phase
In this phase, professional traders start to sell (distribute) their positions to retail traders at higher prices. The [market](../m/market.md) often shows large price movements and increased [volatility](../v/volatility.md) during this phase. High [volume](../v/volume.md) combined with narrow [spreads](../s/spreads.md) or frequent price reversals may indicate professional selling activity.

### 4. Mark-Down Phase
After professional traders have offloaded their positions during the [distribution](../d/distribution.md) phase, prices often begin to fall, leading to the mark-down phase. Retail traders who bought at the higher prices now face a declining [market](../m/market.md), while professionals may start the cycle again by accumulating positions at lower prices.

## Key Patterns in VSA

### 1. Climax Bars
Climax bars are significant price bars associated with extremely high [volume](../v/volume.md), representing exhaustive buying or selling climaxes. These bars often mark the end or beginning of trends and can be used as potential [reversal](../r/reversal.md) signals.

### 2. No Demand Bars
No [demand](../d/demand.md) bars are characterized by narrow [spreads](../s/spreads.md) accompanied by low [volume](../v/volume.md) during an [uptrend](../u/uptrend.md). They indicate a lack of buying [interest](../i/interest.md) and can precede a potential [reversal](../r/reversal.md) or a decline in the current [trend](../t/trend.md).

### 3. No Supply Bars
No [supply](../s/supply.md) bars occur during downtrends or pullbacks and are identified by narrow [spreads](../s/spreads.md) and low [volume](../v/volume.md). They signify a lack of selling pressure and can provide bullish signals for subsequent price increases.

### 4. Test Bars
Test bars appear during a [downtrend](../d/downtrend.md) or after a [correction](../c/correction.md) within an [uptrend](../u/uptrend.md). They feature narrow [spreads](../s/spreads.md) and low [volume](../v/volume.md), suggesting that professionals are testing the [market](../m/market.md) for the presence of [weak hands](../w/weak_hands.md). Successful tests can lead to upward price movements.

### 5. Shakeout Bars
Shakeout bars, or spring bars, show a sudden price drop followed by a sharp recovery on high [volume](../v/volume.md). These bars are designed to shake out retail traders holding long positions with tight stop losses, allowing professionals to accumulate positions at better prices.

## VSA and Technical Indicators

While VSA primarily relies on price, [volume](../v/volume.md), and spread, traders often combine it with [technical indicators](../t/technical_indicators.md) to enhance their analysis and decision-making processes. Examples of such indicators include moving averages, [Relative Strength](../r/relative_strength.md) [Index](../i/index_instrument.md) (RSI), and [Bollinger Bands](../b/bollinger_bands.md).

### Moving Averages
Moving averages smooth out price data to identify trends and reversals. In VSA, moving averages can help confirm the [trend](../t/trend.md) direction during mark-up and mark-down phases.

### Relative Strength Index (RSI)
RSI measures the magnitude of recent price changes to evaluate [overbought](../o/overbought.md) or [oversold](../o/oversold.md) conditions. Using RSI with VSA can help identify potential [reversal](../r/reversal.md) points during climax bars or no [demand](../d/demand.md)/no [supply](../s/supply.md) situations.

### Bollinger Bands
[Bollinger Bands](../b/bollinger_bands.md) consist of a moving average and two [standard deviation](../s/standard_deviation.md) lines. They provide a relative definition of high and low prices. In VSA, [Bollinger Bands](../b/bollinger_bands.md) can highlight [volatility](../v/volatility.md) changes during different phases of the [market](../m/market.md) cycle.
