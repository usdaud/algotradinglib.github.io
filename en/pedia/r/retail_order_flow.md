# Retail Order Flow

Retail order flow refers to the buy and sell orders placed by individual investors rather than institutions. It can influence short term price movements, especially in popular or heavily discussed stocks.

## Characteristics
- Smaller order sizes and higher use of market orders.
- Concentration in certain tickers or themes.
- Sensitivity to news, social media, and sentiment.

## Example
A heavily discussed stock sees a surge in small buy orders from retail traders, pushing the price higher despite limited institutional participation.

## Practical notes
Retail flow can create momentum but can also reverse quickly. Some strategies monitor retail activity indicators to gauge sentiment.

## Practical checklist
- Define the time horizon for Retail Order Flow and the market context.
- Identify the data inputs you trust, such as price, volume, or schedule dates.
- Write a clear entry and exit rule before committing capital.
- Size the position so a single error does not damage the account.
- Document the result to improve repeatability.

## Common pitfalls
- Treating Retail Order Flow as a standalone signal instead of context.
- Ignoring liquidity, spreads, and execution friction.
- Using a rule on a different timeframe than it was designed for.
- Overfitting a small sample of past examples.
- Assuming the same behavior in abnormal volatility.

## Data and measurement
Good analysis starts with consistent data. For Retail Order Flow, confirm the data source, the time zone, and the sampling frequency. If the concept depends on settlement or schedule dates, align the calendar with the exchange rules. If it depends on price action, consider using adjusted data to handle corporate actions.

## Risk management notes
Risk control is essential when applying Retail Order Flow. Define the maximum loss per trade, the total exposure across related positions, and the conditions that invalidate the idea. A plan for fast exits is useful when markets move sharply.
